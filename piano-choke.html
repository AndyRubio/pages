Piano Practice Advice

This is a practice-design problem, not a “you’re bad under pressure” problem.

You have trained your brain that when an error happens, the action is: stop → restart → try again. So in front of your teacher, the same habit fires automatically. You need to train a different reflex: notice mistake → keep pulse → recover at the next stable point.

Here is how to fix it.

1. Practice “continuing” as a separate skill

Take an easy section of the piece and deliberately make tiny mistakes while keeping going.

For example:

  • play one wrong note on purpose
  • miss one dynamic
  • skip a repeated note
  • play a fingering slightly wrong

But the rule is: the beat must not stop.

The goal is not accuracy. The goal is recovery. In an exam, a small wrong note is usually much less damaging than stopping, restarting, or losing the pulse completely.

2. Create “rescue points” in the piece

Mark 3–6 places in the score where you can restart mentally if something goes wrong. These should be places like:

  • the start of a phrase
  • a bar with a clear left-hand pattern
  • a cadence
  • a repeated rhythmic idea
  • the start of a new line

Then practice jumping to each rescue point cold. Don’t always start from the beginning.

You want to be able to think: “I messed up bar 6, but I know I can land safely at bar 8.”

3. Isolate the choke spot, but don’t just repeat it

Repeating the problem bar ten times from a stop often trains panic around that bar.

Use this instead:

Micro-loop:

Play the choke spot extremely slowly, maybe at half speed, with perfect rhythm and relaxed hands.

Entry-loop:

Start 1 bar before the choke spot and continue 1 bar after it.

Exit-loop:

Start inside the choke spot and continue out of it.

Random-start practice:

Ask your teacher, or use a random number, to start from different bars. This prevents your brain from only knowing the piece as “from the beginning.”

4. Practice the piece with “no stopping” rules

Do two different types of run-throughs:

Practice run:

You may stop, analyze, correct, and repeat.

Performance run:

No stopping. No correcting. No facial reaction. Keep going until the end.

Do not mix these. Before you start, decide which type you are doing.

For performance runs, record yourself. Your job is not to play perfectly. Your job is to finish.

5. Make the choke spot boring

The part where you choke probably has too much emotional weight now. You may be anticipating failure before you even get there.

So practice it in low-pressure, slightly silly ways:

  • hands separately
  • very slowly
  • with eyes closed, only if safe
  • staccato instead of legato
  • forte, then piano
  • counting aloud
  • tapping the rhythm on the fallboard
  • playing only the left hand while singing the right-hand rhythm
  • starting from the final note and building backwards

The aim is to make your brain think: “This is familiar,” not “Here comes the scary part.”

6. Use “one correction only”

After a mistake, don’t immediately replay the whole piece.

Use this rule:

  1. Identify the exact cause: wrong note, fingering, rhythm, hand position, looking away, tension, panic.
  2. Fix that cause slowly.
  3. Repeat correctly 3 times.
  4. Move on.

Do not punish the whole piece for one bad bar.

7. Train exam behavior explicitly

For ABRSM Initial Grade, the examiner is not expecting concert-level perfection. They are looking for steady pulse, musical awareness, basic control, and recovery. A stumble is survivable. A full derailment is more costly.

So rehearse exactly what you will do after a mistake:

  • keep counting
  • keep the left hand going if possible
  • simplify briefly if needed
  • rejoin at the next beat/bar/rescue point
  • do not apologise
  • do not restart unless instructed

A useful mantra: wrong note, right time.

8. What to do this week

For each practice session:

  1. Spend 3 minutes on the choke spot slowly.
  2. Spend 3 minutes entering and exiting the choke spot.
  3. Do one full no-stopping performance run.
  4. Write down where the performance nearly broke.
  5. Fix only that small area.
  6. End by playing something easy well.

The key shift: stop trying to prove you can play the piece correctly. Start training the ability to recover while the music continues. That is what exam performance actually requires.
