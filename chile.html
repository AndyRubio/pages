7 Days in Santiago, Chile

A one-week plan mixing city life, mountains, wine country, and the Pacific coast.

Assumes a September trip (late winter / early spring): cool in the city, cold in the Andes. Pack layers.

Overview

  • Days 1–3: Historic Santiago, viewpoints, museums, and classic neighbourhoods.
  • Day 4: Andes excursion to Cajón del Maipo & Embalse El Yeso (or similar high-Andes trip).
  • Day 5: Maipo Valley wine region.
  • Day 6: Valparaíso & Viña del Mar on the coast.
  • Day 7: Modern Santiago, parks, and last-minute shopping.

Reality check: mountain roads can close due to snow or landslides, and air quality in Santiago can be hazy. Always confirm conditions locally a day or two before each trip.

Day 1 – Historic Centre & Barrio Lastarria

Morning: Plaza de Armas & La Moneda

  • Walk the Plaza de Armas: Cathedral, street life, and historic buildings.
  • Visit the Metropolitan Cathedral and the National History Museum if you’re up for a first museum.
  • Stroll down Paseo Ahumada towards the La Moneda Palace (presidential palace). If timing works, catch the changing of the guard (not daily, check schedule).

Afternoon: Cerro Santa Lucía & Coffee

  • Climb Cerro Santa Lucía for terraces, fountains, and city views. It’s short but has some steep, uneven steps.
  • Grab a coffee nearby, then wander into Barrio Lastarria, a compact area filled with bookshops, bars, and galleries.

Evening: Dinner in Lastarria

  • Eat in one of Lastarria’s restaurants (good options for Chilean, fusion, and wine bars).
  • Walk past the Centro Cultural Gabriela Mistral (GAM); often there’s live music, performances, or at least a lively plaza.

Day 2 – Bellavista, Cerro San Cristóbal & Nightlife

Morning: Barrio Bellavista

  • Head to Bellavista, known for murals, bars, and Pablo Neruda’s house La Chascona (reserve ahead if you want a tour).
  • Explore side streets and grab brunch in a café around Patio Bellavista. It’s touristy but convenient.

Afternoon: Cerro San Cristóbal

  • Take the funicular or cable car up Cerro San Cristóbal for one of the best views of Santiago. On clear days you’ll see the Andes lined up behind the skyline.
  • Walk around the summit area (sanctuary and viewpoints). September can still be chilly and windy up top.
  • Descend on the opposite side by cable car if open, and wander through the Metropolitan Park.

Evening: Bellavista After Dark

  • Return to Bellavista for dinner and drinks.
  • Keep an eye on your belongings at night; it’s lively, but petty theft is a thing here like in most big cities.

Day 3 – Museums & Neighbourhoods

Morning: Pre-Columbian Art & Downtown

  • Visit the Museo Chileno de Arte Precolombino: compact, high-quality exhibits; easy to fit into a couple of hours.
  • Optional: short stop at the Museo de Bellas Artes just across the river near Lastarria.

Afternoon: Memory Museum & Barrio Yungay

  • Take the metro/taxi to the Museo de la Memoria y los Derechos Humanos (Museum of Memory and Human Rights). It’s powerful and heavy, but important for understanding modern Chile.
  • Walk around nearby Barrio Yungay, with old mansions, murals, and quieter streets than downtown.

Evening: Barrio Italia or Back to Lastarria

  • For design shops and restaurants, head to Barrio Italia (converted houses, courtyards, and boutiques).
  • Or return to Lastarria if you prefer somewhere familiar with more nightlife.

Day 4 – Cajón del Maipo & Embalse El Yeso (Andes Day Trip)

This is a long day but one of the most impressive, taking you into the Andes southeast of Santiago.

Full Day: High-Andes Scenery

  • Join a guided tour to Cajón del Maipo and Embalse El Yeso. They usually start around 6–7 am and last 10–11 hours.
  • Expect mountain roads, viewpoints, and a walk near the turquoise reservoir if access is open.
  • Many tours include a simple picnic and a few photo stops; check exactly what’s covered.
  • Bring warm layers, hat, gloves, sunscreen, and water. Weather can shift quickly and it can be below freezing even if Santiago feels mild.

If access to Embalse El Yeso is closed (road damage, snow, or regulations), look for alternative Andes tours – for example, trips to mountain villages or ski areas like Valle Nevado. Ask agencies what’s currently operating rather than assuming.

Day 5 – Maipo Valley Wine Country

Full Day: Wineries & Countryside

  • Book a Maipo Valley wine tour (group or private). Most leave mid-morning and visit 2–3 wineries.
  • Typical stops include tastings of Chilean reds (Cabernet Sauvignon, Carmenere) and cellar visits.
  • Some tours include lunch at a vineyard restaurant; others give free time in a town like Isla de Maipo. Check details so you’re not surprised by extra costs.
  • Don’t drive yourself if you plan on drinking. Either join a tour with transfers or hire a driver. Local enforcement on drink driving is strict, as it should be.

If you’re not into alcohol, there are still options: vineyard tours with grape juice, olive oil tastings, or swapping this day for extra hiking or thermal springs.

Day 6 – Valparaíso & Viña del Mar (Pacific Coast)

Morning: Drive to the Coast & Viña del Mar

  • Take an early bus or tour (about 1.5–2 hours) to the coast.
  • Start in Viña del Mar: sea views, the famous flower clock, and seaside promenades.
  • If time allows, check out Wulff Castle or the Fonck Museum (with an Easter Island moai at the entrance).

Afternoon: Valparaíso Hills & Street Art

  • Move on to Valparaíso, the gritty port city covered in murals.
  • Focus on Cerro Alegre and Cerro Concepción: funiculars, viewpoints, cafes, and galleries.
  • Consider a walking tour to get context on the art and history; it’s easy to wander aimlessly here.

Valparaíso is fascinating but rough around the edges. Avoid flashing valuables, stick to busy routes on the hills, and don’t push your luck walking through the lower port area after dark.

Evening: Return to Santiago

  • Head back to Santiago by early evening by bus or with your tour group.
  • Light dinner near your accommodation – you’ll probably be tired from all the walking.

Day 7 – Modern Santiago, Parks & Last-Minute Shopping

Morning: Costanera & Sky View

  • Head to the Costanera Center in Providencia: huge mall plus the Sky Costanera observation deck.
  • Go up on a clear morning for 360° views; you’ll recognise many places you’ve visited during the week.
  • Use the mall for any practical shopping you still need (outdoor gear, souvenirs, snacks for the flight).

Afternoon: Parks & Relaxing

  • Take a taxi or bus to Parque Bicentenario in Vitacura: ponds, walkways, and often flamingos.
  • Have a long lunch in Vitacura or nearby Las Condes (lots of mid-to-upscale restaurants).

Evening: Farewell Dinner

  • Pick your favourite neighbourhood from the week (Lastarria, Bellavista, Italia, or Providencia) for a final dinner.
  • Try dishes you haven’t had yet: pastel de choclo, cazuela, or more seafood if it’s been good to you.

Practical Notes

  • Transport: Get a rechargeable transport card (currently Bip!/TÜP) for metro and buses. Taxis and ride-hailing apps are widely used.
  • Altitude & weather: Santiago itself is manageable, but Andes trips mean higher altitude and colder temperatures. If you feel off, take it seriously.
  • Safety: Normal big-city precautions. Avoid using your phone carelessly on busy streets and keep valuables close in crowded areas and on public transport.
  • Planning: Book major tours (Cajón del Maipo, wine tours, coast day trip) ahead of time, especially on weekends. Check cancellation policies – mountain weather can force changes.
